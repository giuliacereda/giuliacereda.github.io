What exactly is the Likelihood Ratio?

Posted on February 20, 2019

In the previous post we introduced forensic statistics as the science of evaluating the probative value of evidence. One of the most important tools for doing so is the Likelihood Ratio (LR).

The idea is simple: when faced with a piece of evidence, we compare two competing explanations – typically the hypothesis of the prosecution (e.g. "the DNA comes from the suspect") and the hypothesis of the defence (e.g. "the DNA comes from someone else").

The Likelihood Ratio tells us how many times more likely the evidence is under one hypothesis compared to the other.

For example, suppose a DNA profile from a crime scene matches a suspect. If such a match would occur in 1 out of a million unrelated individuals, the evidence is a million times more likely under the prosecution’s hypothesis than under the defence’s. In this case, the LR is 1,000,000.

Importantly, the LR does not tell us whether the suspect is guilty or not. Rather, it quantifies how much the evidence shifts the balance between the two competing hypotheses. It is then up to the court to combine this information with all other elements of the case.

The strength of the LR approach lies in its transparency: it makes explicit the assumptions, allows clear communication of the evidential weight, and can be applied consistently across different types of forensic data.
